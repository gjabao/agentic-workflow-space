# SOFI Buyer Persona Research
**Client:** Source of Fabric International (SOFI), Los Angeles fabric supplier

**Purpose:** Inform cold email targeting via Instantly campaigns

## Market Overview: LA Apparel Ecosystem

Los Angeles is the largest apparel manufacturing hub in the United States, with over **4,000+ apparel companies** and hundreds of fabric showrooms concentrated in the Fashion District and surrounding areas. California accounts for roughly 20% of all U.S. apparel manufacturing output.

### Geographic Concentration of Target Accounts

| Area | What's There | Relevance to SOFI |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| DTLA Fashion District (9th St corridor) | Fabric showrooms, cut-and-sew factories, trim suppliers, pattern makers | Highest concentration of fabric buyers; walk-in and same-day sourcing culture |
| Arts District / East LA | Emerging indie designers, DTC studios, creative agencies | Price-conscious but quality-aware founders |
| El Monte / Vernon / City of Commerce | Mid-market and volume manufacturers, private label factories | Production-scale buyers with recurring MOQ needs |
| West Hollywood / Silver Lake | Premium indie brands, celebrity-backed labels, sustainable designers | Values-driven sourcing, willing to pay for provenance |
| Culver City / Santa Monica | Tech-forward DTC brands, brand studios with in-house design | Online-first brands scaling to physical production |

## Company Profiles: 5 Types of SOFI Customers

### Type 1 — Indie / Emerging Designer
- **Size:** 1–5 employees, pre-revenue to $500K/year
- **Stage:** First or second collection, often direct-to-consumer via Instagram/Shopify
- **Sourcing:** Irregular, reactive, low volume (50–300 yards per order)
- **MOQ sensitivity:** Extremely high — will reject suppliers requiring 500+ yard minimums
- **Relationship:** Founder does everything including sourcing

### Type 2 — DTC Growth Brand
- **Size:** 5–30 employees, $500K–$5M annual revenue
- **Stage:** Post-product-market-fit, scaling SKUs and production runs
- **Sourcing:** Seasonal with some carry-forward basics; 300–2,000 yards per fabric
- **MOQ sensitivity:** Moderate — can hit $5K–$20K per order, wants flexibility
- **Relationship:** Founder or dedicated production coordinator handles vendors

### Type 3 — Private Label Manufacturer / CMT Shop
- **Size:** 10–100 employees, $1M–$15M revenue
- **Stage:** Established; produces for multiple brand clients
- **Sourcing:** Ongoing, high-volume, category-focused (knits, wovens, specialty)
- **MOQ sensitivity:** Low — actively wants bulk pricing and consistent supply
- **Relationship:** Dedicated sourcing manager or production director

### Type 4 — Mid-Market Brand (Established)
- **Size:** 30–200 employees, $5M–$50M revenue
- **Stage:** Multi-channel (wholesale + DTC), seasonally driven
- **Sourcing:** 6–12 month forward planning, approved vendor lists, multi-category
- **MOQ sensitivity:** Low — sophisticated procurement, price-per-yard negotiation
- **Relationship:** VP of Sourcing or Sourcing Director, formal RFP process

### Type 5 — Uniform / Workwear / Promotional Brand
- **Size:** 5–50 employees, $500K–$10M revenue
- **Stage:** Niche B2B apparel (hospitality, healthcare, corporate merch)
- **Sourcing:** Specification-driven (performance fabric, durability standards), recurring
- **MOQ sensitivity:** Low for basics, high for specialty
- **Relationship:** Owner or operations manager

## Buyer Persona Profiles

---

### PERSONA 1 — "The Grinding Founder"
**Name (fictional):** Maya Chen
**Age:** 28–38
**Title:** Founder / Creative Director
**Company type:** Indie DTC brand, 2–10 employees
**Revenue:** $200K–$2M/year
**Location:** Arts District, Silver Lake, or West Hollywood

#### Background
Maya left a corporate fashion job (or a design school program) to launch her own label. She handles design, sourcing, production coordination, marketing, and customer service — often simultaneously. She sources fabric reactively, season by season, often scrambling close to production deadlines.

#### Goals
- Find reliable fabric sources she can reorder from without chasing lead times
- Discover unique or trending fabrics that differentiate her designs
- Keep COGs low enough to maintain 60%+ margins on DTC pricing
- Build a brand story she can tell on Instagram (fabric origin matters)

#### Frustrations
- Minimums are too high for her run sizes (she needs 100–300 yards, not 1,000)
- Fabric arrives late, killing her production schedule
- She found a great fabric at a trade show but can't reorder it 6 months later
- Overseas sourcing has unpredictable lead times (18–20 weeks kills her seasonal calendar)
- Suppliers ghost her when order sizes are small

#### What Makes Her Switch Suppliers
- A supplier who can hit her MOQ without upcharging
- Someone who texts her back same day (relationship and responsiveness > price)
- A new unique fabric she can't find elsewhere (creative pull)
- Referral from another founder she trusts

#### Buying Behavior
- Finds suppliers at **LA Textile Show** (twice yearly at California Market Center)
- **Instagram** — follows fabric suppliers, mills, and fellow designers
- **Referrals** from her network (designer Slack groups, DTLA factory contacts)
- Occasional Google search when she has a specific need ("recycled nylon jersey LA")
- Does NOT respond well to generic sales emails; responds to personalization

#### Communication Channel Preference
- Instagram DM (discovery)
- Email (follow-up and samples)
- Text/WhatsApp (ongoing relationship)
- Rarely LinkedIn

#### Budget & Order Patterns
- $500–$5,000 per fabric order
- 2–4 orders per season, 2–3 seasons per year
- Grows 30–50% year-over-year if brand is gaining traction

#### What She Cares About (in order)
1. Responsiveness and relationship
2. Uniqueness of the fabric (creative edge)
3. Minimum order flexibility
4. Price per yard
5. Sustainability / origin story (increasingly important for her brand)

#### Cold Email Hook Angle (for SOFI)
> "Found a fabric you can actually reorder" — reliability and low-MOQ access
> Connector angle: intro from someone in her network, or reference to a specific fabric type she's known for

### PERSONA 2 — "The Operational Scaler"
**Name (fictional):** Daniel Reyes
**Age:** 32–45
**Title:** Production Manager / Head of Production / Sourcing & Production Coordinator
**Company type:** Growth-stage DTC or wholesale brand
**Revenue:** $2M–$15M/year
**Location:** DTLA, Culver City, or remote (hybrid)

#### Background
Daniel was hired specifically to take production off the founder's plate. He has 5–10 years of apparel production experience, possibly from a larger brand or a manufacturer. He manages a vendor roster, coordinates with overseas factories, and sources domestically when speed is needed. He talks to fabric suppliers weekly.

#### Goals
- Build a reliable approved vendor list he can go back to season after season
- Reduce lead times on core fabrics (basics and carry-forwards)
- Hit cost targets set by the CFO or founder while maintaining quality
- Avoid production shutdowns caused by late or incorrect fabric delivery

#### Frustrations
- Fabric quality inconsistency between orders (dye lot variation, weight differences)
- Suppliers who promise lead times they can't hit
- Poor communication mid-order (no proactive updates)
- Chasing invoices, COAs, and lab test results from vendors
- Finding a new supplier for a specialty fabric when his current one runs out of stock

#### What Makes Him Switch Suppliers
- Current supplier misses a delivery and delays production
- A competitor brand's production manager tells him about a better source
- SOFI offers domestic stock of a fabric he currently sources from overseas (speed advantage)
- Transparent pricing with no surprise upcharges

#### Buying Behavior
- Evaluates suppliers formally: samples → lab test → small order → scale
- Attends **Texworld LA** and **LA Textile Show** as part of his job
- Uses **LinkedIn** to vet suppliers (checks company page, reviews)
- Relies heavily on **referrals within the industry** (tight-knit LA production community)
- Responds to email if it's specific and relevant; deletes generic outreach

#### Communication Channel Preference
- **Email** (primary — he manages vendors via email)
- **LinkedIn** (for vetting / initial discovery)
- Phone calls for urgent/in-progress issues
- Rarely Instagram

#### Budget & Order Patterns
- $5,000–$50,000 per fabric order
- 6–20 orders per year across multiple fabric categories
- Wants blanket POs or standing orders for core fabrics
- Has a budget approval threshold (often can approve up to $10K independently; above requires sign-off)

#### What He Cares About (in order)
1. Delivery reliability (on-time, right quality)
2. Communication and responsiveness
3. Price competitiveness vs. alternatives
4. Consistency across reorders
5. Sustainability certifications (if brand has a sustainability commitment)

#### Cold Email Hook Angle (for SOFI)
> "Domestic stock means you hit your production schedule" — lead time certainty
> Pain signal: company is posting job ads for production roles (scaling) or recently launched new categories
> Connector angle: reference a mutual contact at a factory or another brand

### PERSONA 3 — "The Volume Buyer"
**Name (fictional):** Kevin Park
**Age:** 38–55
**Title:** Owner / VP Operations / Sourcing Director
**Company type:** Private label manufacturer, CMT shop, or mid-market established brand
**Revenue:** $5M–$40M/year
**Location:** El Monte, Vernon, City of Commerce, or DTLA core

#### Background
Kevin runs a CMT (Cut-Make-Trim) operation or a private label manufacturer that produces for multiple brand clients. He buys fabric in large quantities, negotiates hard on price, and has existing vendor relationships — but is always evaluating alternatives for cost, availability, and specialty categories his current suppliers don't cover.

#### Goals
- Consistent supply of high-volume basics (knit jerseys, wovens, linings) at competitive cost
- Access to specialty fabrics for clients who want unique materials
- Reduce dependency on overseas fabric imports (tariff exposure, lead time risk)
- Vendor who can accommodate split shipments and flexible scheduling

#### Frustrations
- Overseas suppliers affected by tariffs, shipping delays, and port congestion
- Fabric mills with high MOQs that don't match his client order sizes
- Price volatility from overseas (currency, freight surcharges)
- Finding a domestic supplier who can compete on price with imports for volume orders

#### What Makes Him Switch Suppliers
- Current overseas supplier is disrupted (tariffs, geopolitical risk — a live issue in 2025–2026)
- SOFI offers a domestically stocked fabric that undercuts import landed cost + lead time
- A client specifically requests a fabric SOFI carries
- Better payment terms (Net 30/60 vs. upfront payment required by overseas mills)

#### Buying Behavior
- Already has 3–8 fabric suppliers on rotation
- Evaluates new suppliers via **price quote + sample** before anything else
- Referral-driven: trusts peers in the manufacturing community
- Attends trade shows but mostly as a buyer refresher, not as primary discovery
- May respond to LinkedIn InMail if message is highly specific to his category

#### Communication Channel Preference
- **Direct phone or in-person** (prefers relationship-based selling)
- **Email** for formal quotes and orders
- **LinkedIn** for initial outreach only if message is sharp
- Very resistant to cold outreach — needs warm intro or very targeted hook

#### Budget & Order Patterns
- $20,000–$200,000+ per fabric order
- Ongoing, recurring purchases across 3–6 fabric categories
- Wants volume pricing tiers and the ability to call-off against blanket orders
- Long evaluation cycle (2–4 months from first contact to first real order)

#### What He Cares About (in order)
1. Price per yard at volume
2. Consistent stock availability (no stockouts mid-production)
3. Lead time (domestic vs. import comparison)
4. Supplier financial stability / reliability
5. Minimum requirements and flexibility

#### Cold Email Hook Angle (for SOFI)
> "Domestic alternative to your current overseas source — before the next tariff round hits"
> Signal-based: company is importing fabric from China/SE Asia (check Panjiva/ImportGenius for shipping data)
> Connector angle: mutual contact at a factory or through a fabric trade association

## Supplier Evaluation Criteria (what they check before buying)

1. **Sample quality** — always. No one buys blind.
2. **MOQ** — screener #1 for small brands
3. **Lead time** — domestic stock vs. import lead time is a major differentiator for SOFI
4. **Price per yard** — compared vs. current supplier
5. **Reorder consistency** — same dye lot, weight, and hand feel every time
6. **Sustainability credentials** — increasingly important (OEKO-TEX, recycled content, organic cert)
7. **Responsiveness** — how fast do they respond to sample requests and questions?
8. **Payment terms** — Net 30 preferred; prepayment is a barrier for small brands

## Cold Email Sequencing Recommendations

### For Persona 1 (Maya):
- **Subject line angle:** Reference a specific fabric type she uses (sourced from her Instagram)
- **Hook:** Pain around MOQ or lead time flexibility
- **CTA:** "Can I send you a sample of X?"
- **Channel order:** Instagram engagement → DM → Email follow-up

### For Persona 2 (Daniel):
- **Subject line angle:** Production schedule certainty, domestic stock
- **Hook:** "We stock [specific fabric] domestically — 5-day lead time vs. your current import timeline"
- **CTA:** "Worth a quick call to see if our stock matches your specs?"
- **Channel order:** LinkedIn view → Cold email → Follow-up with sample offer

### For Persona 3 (Kevin):
- **Subject line angle:** Import disruption risk, tariff exposure
- **Hook:** Signal-based (detect their import activity) + pricing comparison offer
- **CTA:** "Let me send you a quote on [specific category] — might be worth benchmarking"
- **Channel order:** Referral intro preferred → if cold, LinkedIn InMail → Email with specific quote offer
