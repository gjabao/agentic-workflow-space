# SOFI (Source of Fabric International) — Complete ICP Profile

**Synthesized from 4-agent fan-out research | March 30, 2026**

---

## Executive Summary

SOFI is positioned in the strongest domestic fabric sourcing environment in a decade. The convergence of 25–49% tariffs on Asian textiles, post-COVID supply chain trauma, and Michael Levine exiting wholesale has created a gap no LA competitor currently fills: **low MOQ + domestic speed + custom printing**. SOFI's ICP is the growing LA apparel brand caught between needing premium fabric and being too small for traditional mills.

---

## Part 1: The Market Landscape

### Why Now

| Macro Trend | Impact on SOFI |
|---|---|
| US tariffs on Chinese textiles: 25%+ | "Overseas is cheaper" objection is dead. Domestic sourcing is now price-competitive |
| Vietnam tariff: 46%, Cambodia: 49%, Bangladesh: 37% | Southeast Asia alternatives also hit hard |
| Michael Levine exits wholesale (2025–2026) | Largest LA fabric wholesaler leaves a gap in mid-market B2B buyers |
| LA micro-drop/DTC culture requires speed | 90–120 day overseas lead times are incompatible with weekly drops |
| Immigration enforcement disrupting LA factories (June 2025+) | Brands want supply chain control, not CMT dependency |

### Competitive Position

**SOFI's unique triple:** Low MOQ (25 yd) + Domestic speed (days, not months) + Custom printing

No other LA competitor offers all three:
- **Ben Textiles** — broad selection, no printing
- **Eagle Fabrics** — knit-only, no printing
- **Fairway Fabrics** — premium priced ($10+/yd), Berry Amendment focused
- **SwatchOn** — no MOQ but no physical presence, no printing, no relationship
- **China/Alibaba** — cheapest unit cost but 4–12 week lead + 25%+ tariff + quality risk

## Part 2: Ideal Customer Profile

### Firmographic Criteria

| Attribute | Primary ICP | Secondary ICP |
|---|---|---|
| **Company type** | Indie DTC brand, streetwear label, emerging designer | Private label manufacturer, CMT shop |
| **Stage** | 1–5 years old, post-launch, pre-scale | Established, multi-brand production |
| **Revenue** | $200K–$15M | $5M–$40M |
| **Employees** | 1–30 | 10–100 |
| **Location** | DTLA Fashion District, Arts District, Silver Lake, West Hollywood | El Monte, Vernon, City of Commerce |
| **Production model** | Small-batch, seasonal drops, DTC + emerging wholesale | Volume production for multiple brand clients |
| **Current sourcing** | Mix of overseas + LA Fashion District walk-ins | Import-dependent, China/SE Asia heavy |
| **Order size** | 100–500 yards, $500–$50K/order | 500–5,000+ yards, $20K–$200K/order |

### Disqualifiers

- Already has locked-in domestic supplier they're happy with (no switching signal)
- Fully vertical (owns their own textile production)
- Pure luxury couture (needs bespoke mills, not wholesale)
- Drop-shipping / print-on-demand only (no fabric purchasing)

## Part 4: The 5 Killer Pain Points

Ranked by frequency and intensity across all research:

| # | Pain Point | Severity | Cold Email Hook |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **MOQ Hell** — Mills want 500–1,000m. Brands need 50–200. | 10/10 | "Most fabric suppliers won't talk to you until you need 500+ meters." |
| 2 | **Lead Time Roulette** — Supplier says 30 days, delivers in 60. Entire launch calendar collapses. | 9/10 | "When your fabric supplier misses a delivery date, your entire launch falls apart." |
| 3 | **Dye Lot Inconsistency** — Sample approved, production fabric looks different. $7B+ industry-wide cost. | 9/10 | "You approved the sample. Production fabric looked different. Your customer sees it too." |
| 4 | **Tariff Shock** — Average US apparel import tariffs: 14.7% → 26.4%. Vietnam: 46%. | 9/10 | "Tariffs adding 25%+ to your fabric costs? We print domestically." |
| 5 | **Discontinued Fabric Nightmare** — Collection built around a fabric that gets killed. 8 weeks to launch. | 8/10 | "The fabric you built your collection around just got discontinued." |

**Bonus pain:** LA Fashion District culture is hostile to small/new buyers — multiple reviews cite dismissiveness toward low-volume orders. SOFI can win by simply treating small brands like real customers.
